Fluoride's Tooth-Saving Magic

While the bristles of your toothbrush clean away debris and plaque with each brush, fluoride works silently yet steadily strengthening the tiny crystals within your enamel. Fluoride helps make cavities and cracks hesitate before launching their foul attack.
With each meal or snack, the acids arise to assault your teeth, but fluoride stands vigilant guard. Reinforcements in the form of fluoride arrive to bolster your enamel's defenses before damage can devastate.
Fluoride shields your teeth from acid damage, fortifying the hard outer layer of each tooth against the caustic onslaught of foods and drinks. Though you can't see it at work, you can see and feel the results - strong, shiny teeth that never cavort with decay.
Fluoride is nature's dental champion, using its mineral might to render tooth decay virtually powerless against the forces of protection it provides. When you brush with fluoride, rinse with fluoride, and receive fluoride treatments from your dentist, you are arming your teeth with a defense so formidable, holes and cracks cannot penetrate.
A fluoride defense keeps your teeth healthy, bright, and beautiful for life. It deserves more than a few nights per week or once in a blue moon - ideally, it warrants dedication and consistency. Make it a priority and remember with each brush and swish, you're building a bulwark against dental damage so your smile stays smile-worthy for years to come.

Brush, coat, protect Brush teeth at least twice a day with a soft bristled brush and fluoride toothpaste. Gently brush all tooth surfaces, especially where teeth meet gums. Floss daily to remove food and plaque between teeth.
Use fluoride mouthwash or rinse once per day to boost protection. Fluoride strengthens tooth enamel against acid attacks. See your dentist for professional fluoride treatments and cleanings every 6 months.

Root canals explained Root canals remove infection and decay from inside the tooth, sterilizing and preparing it to last a lifetime with the right care and crown. After numbing and opening the tooth, dentists eliminate diseased or dead pulp (blood vessels and nerves) then clean, shape and seal the root canals to prevent reinfection. Should infection persist, antibiotics may assist before restoring the tooth.
